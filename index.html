Imitation Learning for Soft Robot Navigation

🏆 First Prize — Medical Robotics System Design course

Developed an imitation learning framework to enable a soft guidewire to navigate complex environments by learning from expert demonstrations.

Machine LearningPythonSoft Robotics
Details ›

Overview: This project addresses autonomous navigation for soft continuum guidewires, whose nonlinear and deformable dynamics make traditional model-based control difficult. We applied imitation learning to learn control policies directly from expert demonstrations, enabling reliable navigation without explicit dynamic modeling.

Design & Process: The project followed three phases: Phase 1 designed and built the experimental setup with linear and bending actuators and a maze environment. Phase 2 collected expert demonstrations using QR-calibrated cameras, recording target direction, guidewire positions, syringe actuation, and control inputs. Phase 3 trained an imitation learning model on this data and evaluated autonomous navigation performance.

Results: The guidewire successfully navigated the maze in 10 out of 12 trials, demonstrating robust task execution. The system also included error correction, adjusting its trajectory when initially moving in the wrong direction. This performance contributed to winning First Prize in the Medical Robotics System Design course.

Robotic Laser Surface Ablation

UR5e robotic system performs precise laser ablation on 3D surfaces.

RoboticsROS2ControlComputer VisionPath Planning
Details ›

Overview: This project demonstrates a UR5e robotic system for precise surface treatment on 2D and 3D models. The robot maintains a constant distance from the target area to ensure uniform energy delivery. The lab project integrates robotic motion, vision-based surface detection, and controlled laser operation.

Design & Process: The workflow began with CAD modeling of the robotic setup and a 3D-printed arm model. AprilTags were used to detect and register the surface frame, while the UR5e arm was programmed to follow the treatment area with normal laser incidence. The system was first validated on a 2D plane before applying the same approach to the 3D model. Final implementation integrated path planning, robotic control, and vision-based registration to achieve precise and repeatable laser ablation.

Results: The team successfully registered the arm model using a combination of AprilTags and homography, planned the laser trajectory, and maintained a constant distance for treatment. The system achieved 0.4 mm average error (1.9 mm RMS), meeting predefined precision specifications.

Solar Tracking Panel

Designed a portable solar tracking system that automatically aligns a photovoltaic panel with the sun to improve energy harvesting for small-scale and off-grid applications.

Mechanical Design Control Systems Product Design Prototyping Engineering Design
Details ›

Overview: This project developed a compact and affordable solar tracking platform intended for residential and portable energy use. The goal was to bridge the gap between large, expensive tracking systems and small fixed solar panels by creating a lightweight system capable of improving energy generation while remaining easy to deploy and maintain.

Design & Process: The team followed a structured engineering design workflow beginning with market, patent, and customer research to define requirements for size, cost, weight, maintenance, and power output. Functional decomposition was used to separate key subsystems including frame rotation, tracking mechanism, and panel mounting. Multiple design concepts were generated and evaluated using a Pugh decision matrix, leading to a distributed actuator configuration that improved simplicity and manufacturability. A bill of materials and sourcing plan were then developed to satisfy constraints of under 20 lb total weight and a manufacturing cost below $100.

Results: The final design achieved an autonomous solar tracking concept capable of following sunlight while meeting predefined customer and engineering requirements. The estimated production cost was approximately $65 with a projected retail price of $100, demonstrating feasibility as an accessible mid-range solar solution. Risk assessment and validation planning supported advancement toward the critical design phase.

Both methods operate in a receding-horizon loop, sampling candidate control sequences and evaluating them against a quadratic cost function to drive the quadrotor from rest to a desired goal position.

Stereo Vision Object Tracking

This project implements a stereo vision system that combines camera calibration with YOLO-based object detection to measure real-world distances to objects. Using a pair of calibrated stereo cameras, it rectifies image pairs to align epipolar lines, runs YOLO to detect and track objects in the scene, and then computes depth by matching detections across the left and right camera views. The result is a real-time system that can identify objects and estimate how far away they are.
